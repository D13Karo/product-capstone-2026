### 4. Problem Exploration Narrative
Nana provided a perspective on the "second-order" effects of bad data. While other students focus on their personal schedule, Nana cares about the **survival of the group**. She explained that every time a game is poorly coordinated, it drains the group's social energy. 

She detailed a volleyball match in March where a "maybe cancel?" message led to half the team staying home while the other half showed up to an empty court. This "failed coordination" creates a cycle of apathy. Nana has become an **unofficial confirmation relay**, manually texting individuals to make sure games happen, which she described as an exhausting and unsustainable workaround.

### 5. Mom Test Compliance Check
* **Pass:** I dug into her history with groups that no longer exist. By asking, "What was the specific turning point for the group that stopped playing?" I found that the issue wasn't lack of interest in sports, but "logistical fatigue."
* **Fail:** I spent too much time in the "empathy trap," agreeing with her that it's "sad" when groups fall apart instead of asking for more specific numbers.
* **Correction:** I should have asked: "In the week that the group dissolved, how many times did an organizer post the wrong information?" to get a concrete failure rate.

### 6. Personal Reflection
Nana taught me that the "pain" of this problem is cumulative. It’s not just about one missed game; it’s about the **slow death of a community**. It shifted my thinking: we are building a tool that preserves the social fabric of KIU sports by removing the frustration that makes people want to quit.
