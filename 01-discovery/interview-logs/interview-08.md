## The Story

Nana is the team heart. She plays sports primarily for the social connection — she talks about her teammates more than the games themselves. Her frustration with the information problem is less about missing games personally and more about what it does to the group.

"When three people show up late or not at all, the whole vibe is off," she said. "We can't play properly, people get frustrated, and then nobody wants to organize next time." She described a cascading effect: one disorganized event leads to lower turnout next time, which leads to lower energy, which leads to the group slowly dissolving. She has seen it happen to two groups already.

She told a story about a volleyball game in March where only 4 of the 8 expected players showed up. "Two people didn't see the location change, one person thought it was cancelled because she saw a message that said 'maybe cancel?' and nobody followed up, and one person just never got told." The result: they had to call it off. "Four people wasted their afternoon. And it wasn't anyone's fault — the system just failed."

What struck us was how Nana framed the problem as a collective issue, not an individual one. She didn't talk about herself being frustrated; she talked about the group energy suffering. "Sport is supposed to bring people together. But when logistics fail every week, people stop trying."

---

## 3 Key Quotes

1. "When people don't show up, it's not that they don't care — they just didn't know. But it still ruins the game for everyone who did show up."

2. "I've seen two sports groups completely fall apart because organizing kept being chaotic. It starts with missed games and ends with nobody bothering to organize anymore."

3. "One message said 'maybe cancel?' and nobody followed up. One person saw that and assumed it was cancelled. She missed the game. That one ambiguous message cost us a player."

## Pain Intensity

**Rating:** 4 / 5

**Evidence for rating:** She has witnessed multiple groups dissolving due to coordination failures, and she connects the information problem directly to long-term social outcomes. Her pain is communal and long-term, which is actually a higher systemic risk than individual missed games.

---

## One-Sentence Insight

Nana reveals that the problem has a decay function — each failed coordination event chips away at group cohesion, and if unresolved, the entire sports community gradually collapses.

---

## Current Workaround

- Personally messages each teammate to confirm attendance before every game
- Acts as unofficial confirmation relay ("are you coming? did you see the update?")
- Follows up on ambiguous messages to get clarity for the group

**Why it's inadequate:** She is doing unpaid coordination work on top of her own participation. This is unsustainable and she gets frustrated when her follow-ups go ignored.

---

## Unexpected Findings

- Group cohesion deterioration is a second-order effect of the problem — not just individual inconvenience but community-level harm
- Ambiguous messages ("maybe cancel?") are as damaging as missing messages — uncertainty causes people to default to not showing up
- She represents a user who would value group-level features (attendance tracking, RSVP) not just schedule notifications
