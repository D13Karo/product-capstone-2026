## Interview Log 02: The Fragmentation Trap

### 1. Metadata
* **Date:** March 26, 2026
* **Duration:** 32 minutes
* **Interviewer:** Davit Karoiani
* **Interviewee Pseudonym:** Gega (KIU-CS-02)
* **Recruitment:** Recruited via the KIU football community; interview conducted via Google Meet.

### 2. ICP Fit Assessment
* **Profile:** 3rd-year CS student at KIU.
* **Evidence:** Participates in football tournaments 2–3 times per month.
* **Pain Point:** Has experienced 3+ incidents of venue/time confusion this semester alone.
* **ICP Match:** **Confirmed.** He is a highly active player who is suffering from information fragmentation.

### 3. Verbatim Quotes
> "The Facebook post says one thing, Messenger says another. I don't know which one to trust so I have to check both."
>
> "We wasted 45 minutes on logistics during a tournament. That's a whole game's worth of time, gone."
>
> "I made my own WhatsApp group to fix it but now there are three places to check instead of two. I made the problem worse."
>
> "I'd rather skip a game than show up stressed and not knowing what's going on."
>
> "Newer players (freshmen) are most disadvantaged because they don't know which channel to trust and don't have the social capital to call the organizer directly."

### 4. Problem Exploration Narrative
Gega’s story is a classic case of **information fragmentation**. He is caught between two "official" platforms (Facebook and Messenger) that are rarely in sync. The most critical takeaway was how this affects the quality of the sport—Gega mentioned that showing up "flustered" from a 45-minute delay ruins the competitive experience and performance. 

Interestingly, Gega tried to solve this by creating a "sub-group" on WhatsApp, but he realized this only added to the "noise." This proves that the problem isn't a lack of chat apps, but the lack of a **hierarchy of information**. His behavior shows that players are desperate for a "source of truth" and will even do extra work (like checking three apps) to find it, yet they still fail.

### 5. Mom Test Compliance Check
* **Pass:** When Gega mentioned his frustration, I didn't ask "Would you like a better app?" Instead, I asked him to **calculate the cost**: "How much time did you actually lose?" This led to the specific data point of "45 minutes wasted during a tournament."
* **Fail:** I let him talk about "newer players" (freshmen) for a few minutes. This was a violation of the Mom Test because he was theorizing about *other* people's problems rather than his own.
* **Correction:** I should have redirected him by asking: "Can you tell me about the last time *you* personally had to help a freshman because the info was wrong?" to keep the data grounded in his direct experience.

### 6. Personal Reflection
I was surprised that Gega admitted his own solution (the WhatsApp group) made the problem worse. It taught me that adding more communication channels is actually **toxic** to the user experience. It changed my thinking: our goal shouldn't be to "add a feature" for chat, but to **replace** the need for chat regarding logistics entirely.
