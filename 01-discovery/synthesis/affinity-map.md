## Cluster Details

### Cluster 1: Information Fragmentation

**Theme:** Sports information at KIU is split across Facebook, Messenger, WhatsApp, and direct DMs. No platform is considered authoritative. Players must check multiple channels before every game and still frequently receive wrong or outdated information.

**Sample insights:**

1. "The Facebook post says one thing, Messenger says another. I don't know which one to trust so I have to check both." (I02 — Gega)
2. "There is no source of truth. Everyone has a different version of the information and nobody knows whose version is right." (I06 — Nata)
3. A venue change posted only in Messenger caused 6 of 10 players to go to the wrong field; game delayed 45 minutes. (I02)
4. Mamuka posts the same update to Facebook, then Messenger, then individual DMs — three steps for every single piece of information. (I05)
5. Nata has a personal notes app, screenshots, and calendar events — and still showed up at the wrong venue. (I06)
6. Nia is in 5 sports-related group chats, cross-references two before passing info to others — both are sometimes wrong. (I04)
7. Cotne's Google Sheets solution went stale within a week because the organizer stopped updating it. (I10)
8. Gega created a WhatsApp sub-group to fix fragmentation; now there are three channels instead of two. (I02)
9. Khatia describes the group chat as "a news feed" — schedule updates are 20% of content, buried under memes and voice notes. (I01)
10. Tiko screenshots the tournament bracket and still misses bracket changes between checks. (I09)

**Initial observation:** Users are not failing because they are careless — they are failing because the information architecture is broken. Adding more channels makes it worse. The solution must consolidate, not add.

---

### Cluster 2: Organizer Burnout

**Theme:** Informal organizers are the single point of failure for their entire sports group. The coordination burden is disproportionate and unsustainable — multiple organizers described considering quitting entirely.

**Sample insights:**

1. "I spend more time communicating about the game than actually playing it. That's backwards." (I05 — Mamuka)
2. Mamuka's weekly routine: post to Facebook → repost to Messenger → DM 6–8 core players → field repeat questions. Total: 45–60 min for a 60-minute game. (I05)
3. He changed the game time by one hour, posted it everywhere, and three people still showed up wrong and blamed him. (I05)
4. "The football is great. The admin work is not." — Mamuka is actively considering quitting as organizer. (I05)
5. Khatia occasionally organizes the women's side of mixed games and faces the exact same problem from the supply side. (I01)
6. Cotne confirmed: the organizer is the adoption bottleneck — Sandro refused to switch platforms even for a working Telegram bot. (I10)
7. Sandro (the key hub for multiple groups) is graduating in June — losing him will make the problem significantly worse. (I10)
8. Mamuka tried a shared spreadsheet; nobody used it because it required players to change their behavior. (I05)
9. "If something just auto-sent a reminder to everyone without me doing anything, that would change my life." (I05 — Mamuka, unprompted)

**Initial observation:** The organizer is the gatekeeper of all information. Any solution that adds even one extra step for the organizer will not be adopted. Automation is not a feature — it is a prerequisite.

### Cluster 3: Notification Noise Paradox

**Theme:** Because group chats mix sports logistics with social banter, players face a binary choice: keep notifications on and drown in noise, or mute and miss critical updates. Both options lead to the same outcome — missed games.

**Sample insights:**

1. "I muted the group because it was too noisy. Then I missed the time change. You can't win either way." (I03 — Giorgi)
2. Giorgi missed a 1-hour time shift; his team played short and lost by 3 points. (I03)
3. Misho does not proactively check the group — waits for push notifications; found out he missed a game from an Instagram story. (I07)
4. "I have notifications on for everything and I STILL miss things. There's just too much happening everywhere." (I04 — Nia)
5. Khatia checks "KIU Ball" 4–5 times per day just to find logistics updates. (I01)
6. Tiko checks the group multiple times per day during tournament weeks and still misses bracket changes. (I09)
7. One ambiguous message ("maybe cancel?") with no follow-up caused a player to assume the game was off — four people wasted their afternoon. (I08 — Nana)
8. "Sports should send me a notification like a calendar event. Just one clear ping and I'll be there." (I03 — Giorgi)
9. "Just ping me directly. Game time, location, done. Why is that so hard?" (I07 — Misho)

**Initial observation:** The root cause is channel contamination — social conversation and logistical information share the same space. The solution must separate these entirely, delivering logistics as a dedicated, low-noise signal.

### Cluster 4: Real Costs of Coordination Failure

**Theme:** The consequences of missed information go far beyond inconvenience — they include damaged social reputations, degraded athletic performance, and lasting emotional distress.

**Sample insights:**

1. "I stopped telling friends about games because I'm embarrassed when things change and I've already invited them." (I01 — Khatia)
2. "My team played without me because of a one-hour shift. We lost. I felt terrible about it for two days." (I03 — Giorgi)
3. Gega would rather skip a game than "show up stressed and not knowing what's going on." (I02)
4. Tiko had mentally prepared for one opponent; a last-minute bracket change with 5 minutes' notice disrupted her warmup and game plan. (I09)
5. Nia told four people to attend volleyball at 2pm; game had moved to 4pm without her knowledge; she spread wrong information to all four. (I04)
6. A volleyball game was cancelled entirely because only 4 of 8 expected players showed up due to a missed location change. (I08 — Nana)
7. Mamuka is personally blamed when players arrive at wrong times, even when he posted the correct information. (I05)
8. Tiko complained directly in the group chat about the disorganization and was ignored — no feedback loop exists. (I09)
9. Gega wasted 45 minutes on logistics during a tournament — "That's a whole game's worth of time, gone." (I02)

**Initial observation:** The pain extends into social identity (reputation), competitive outcomes (performance), and emotional wellbeing (guilt). This elevates the problem from a scheduling inconvenience to a meaningful quality-of-life issue for active students.

### Cluster 5: Failed Workarounds

**Theme:** Every interviewee has tried to solve this problem personally. Every solution has failed — either because it required the organizer to change behavior, or because it captured a static moment in time and became obsolete when information changed.

**Sample insights:**

1. Gega's WhatsApp sub-group added a third channel and made fragmentation worse. (I02)
2. Nata's screenshot-and-notes system (15 min/week investment) still failed when a venue change was posted after her last check. (I06)
3. Cotne's Telegram bot worked technically but died in three weeks — organizer wasn't willing to switch platforms. (I10)
4. Cotne's Google Sheets solution went stale immediately when the organizer stopped updating it. (I10)
5. Mamuka's pinned Messenger post is routinely ignored by players. (I05)
6. Giorgi's buddy system — asking one friend to DM him if anything changes — breaks down when the buddy is distracted. (I03)
7. Khatia texts the organizer directly for confirmation; he doesn't always respond quickly during busy periods. (I01)
8. Nana does unpaid relay work: personally confirms attendance with each teammate before every game. (I08)
9. Cotne now texts Sandro personally before every game — "the most un-scalable solution imaginable." (I10)
10. Two prior technical solutions (Telegram bot + Google Sheets) failed for the same reason: organizer workflow disruption. (I10)

**Initial observation:** Workarounds fail for exactly two reasons: organizer adoption friction, or information volatility. Any viable solution must address both simultaneously.

### Cluster 6: Single Source of Truth as Universal Need

**Theme:** Without being prompted, every interviewee described the same ideal: one place where the organizer posts once and all participants see the same, current information. This convergence across 10 independent interviews is strong validation.

**Sample insights:**

1. "I need a place where the organizer posts once and everyone sees the same thing. Not four group chats with different updates." (I06 — Nata)
2. Giorgi's mental model is a calendar event with a push reminder — not a group conversation. (I03)
3. "Just ping me directly. Game time, location, done." — Misho's minimum viable experience. (I07)
4. "Any solution has to require zero extra work from the organizer. If it's one more thing they have to do, it won't happen." (I10 — Cotne)
5. "If something just auto-sent a reminder to everyone without me doing anything, that would change my life." (I05 — Mamuka)
6. Tiko would switch to a reliable tool immediately and actively recruit others — she is a natural early adopter and evangelist. (I09)
7. Nata described the ideal product unprompted: a single updatable source the organizer controls, with read-only access for players. (I06)
8. Nana would value RSVP and attendance tracking on top of schedule notifications — group-level features, not just individual. (I08)
9. All 10 interviewees articulated some version of the same solution independently. (I01–I10)

**Initial observation:** The SSOT is not a feature — it is the entire product. The convergence across all 10 interviews, without prompting, is the strongest signal in the dataset.

### Cluster 7: Community Decay as Second-Order Effect

**Theme:** Coordination failures are not just individual inconveniences — they erode group cohesion over time and eventually cause sports communities to dissolve entirely. This is the highest-stakes consequence of the problem.

**Sample insights:**

1. "I've seen two sports groups completely fall apart because organizing kept being chaotic. It starts with missed games and ends with nobody bothering to organize anymore." (I08 — Nana)
2. Nana describes a decay function: one failed event → lower turnout → lower energy → nobody wants to organize → group dissolves. (I08)
3. Khatia stopped inviting friends to games — the community stops growing because trusted members stop recruiting. (I01)
4. Mamuka is considering quitting as organizer — if he does, the football group ends immediately. (I05)
5. Sandro (informal hub for multiple groups) is graduating in June — a structural collapse is months away. (I10)
6. Freshman players are most disadvantaged: they don't know which channel is authoritative and lack the social capital to contact the organizer directly. (I02)
7. Tiko's in-chat complaint was ignored — there is no mechanism for the community to flag and fix the problem collectively. (I09)
8. "Sport is supposed to bring people together. But when logistics fail every week, people stop trying." (I08 — Nana)

**Initial observation:** The community decay effect means the cost of inaction compounds over time. This is not a stable problem — it gets worse with every failed coordination event and every organizer who burns out.

## Outliers and Surprises

1. **Misho (I07)** has the lowest pain rating (2/5) but articulated the ideal solution more clearly than most high-pain users: "Just ping me directly." He defines the minimum viable experience — if the solution requires any effort, low-engagement users will not adopt it.

2. **Cotne (I10)** is the only "Lead User" in the dataset — someone who already built and failed two technical solutions. His constraint ("zero extra work from the organizer") is the most actionable single design requirement.

3. **Ambiguous messages** ("maybe cancel?") raised by Nana (I08) are as damaging as missing messages entirely. Uncertainty defaults people to not showing up — the solution must eliminate ambiguity, not just deliver information faster.

4. **Freshman disadvantage** was raised by Gega (I02) but no freshman was directly interviewed. This is a gap in the dataset that should be addressed in future research.

5. **Nia (I04)** is a misinformation amplifier — because she is a social hub, wrong information she receives spreads to 4–5 additional people. The problem has a network effect when it reaches highly connected users.

## Key Takeaway

The affinity mapping process confirmed that all roads lead to the same destination: KIU students need a Single Source of Truth for campus sports logistics, controlled by the organizer, requiring zero extra workflow steps, and delivering clean dedicated notifications to participants. Every cluster either describes the problem this would solve or the reason every existing workaround fails to solve it.
