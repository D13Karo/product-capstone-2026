## Key Findings Summary

### Top 3 Validated Patterns

**Pattern #1: Fragmented Information — No Single Source of Truth**
- Frequency: 10/10 interviews
- Intensity: 4.0/5
- Root Cause: No purpose-built coordination channel forces organizers into multi-platform broadcasting, creating fragmentation and inconsistency

**Pattern #2: Schedule Changes Lost in Chat Noise**
- Frequency: 9/10 interviews
- Intensity: 4.1/5
- Root Cause: General-purpose chat apps cannot prioritize critical event updates over casual conversation — changes are indistinguishable from noise

**Pattern #3: Organizer Is the Single Point of Failure**
- Frequency: 7/10 interviews
- Intensity: 2.9/5 overall (4.5/5 for organizer-adjacent users)
- Root Cause: No low-effort broadcast tool exists, so organizers manually compensate with redundant multi-platform work — burden grows linearly, burnout is inevitable

## Problem Statement

### Final Evidence-Based Statement

KIU students who participate in informal peer-organized sports events consistently miss game time or location changes, arrive unprepared, or show up to cancelled events because schedule updates get buried in the noise of general-purpose group chats (Messenger, Facebook, WhatsApp) and are spread inconsistently across multiple platforms with no single source of truth. During the academic semester, in the 24–48 hours before each game, critical updates compete with casual conversation for attention — with no way to distinguish a cancellation from a meme. As a result, 7 in 10 students we interviewed missed at least one game last semester due to an information failure; group games are frequently cancelled for lack of quorum; informal sports communities gradually dissolve as participants lose confidence that showing up is worth the effort; and the organizers who hold these groups together spend more time on logistics than on playing, with at least one considering quitting. The current workaround — checking multiple chats, taking screenshots, texting the organizer directly — fails systematically: every workaround we documented either required unsustainable effort or made the fragmentation worse.

### Confidence Level

**Team Confidence:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (5/5)

**Rationale:**
All 3 top patterns are validated across 9–10/10 interviews with average pain intensity of 4.0–4.1/5. Every workaround attempted by participants failed, confirming the problem is structural. Two real campus sports groups have already dissolved following this pattern. Quantified impact is strong: 7/10 missed games, 45–60 min/week organizer overhead, 4/8 quorum failure leading to a cancelled game.

## Lessons Learned

### Discovery Process Learnings

**What we learned about customer discovery:**
1. The organizer is not the same user as the participant — the problem has two sides (supply and demand) and a solution must address both
2. Workarounds are product research — each failed attempt (bot, sub-group, screenshot system, mute strategy) is a user-led prototype that tells us exactly what a successful solution must do
3. Even low-motivation users know what good looks like — Misho barely feels the pain but articulated the ideal experience more clearly than many high-pain interviewees

**What we learned about our problem:**
1. Community decay is the real long-term stakes — missing one game is an annoyance, but repeated failures dissolve groups entirely (two groups already gone)
2. Ambiguous messages are as damaging as missing messages — "maybe cancel?" caused a no-show just as effectively as a missed update
3. Organizer burnout is an underrated risk — the 1:1 ratio of logistics time to play time is unsustainable and at least one organizer is considering quitting

**What we learned about our ICP:**
1. The ICP must include organizers, not just participants — they are the primary design target
2. Pain intensity varies by user type: organizers and competitive players feel it most acutely; casual players feel it less but still experience real consequences

### Unexpected Findings

**Surprises:**
- Every self-built workaround made things worse rather than better
- A specific, named coordinator (Sandro) is graduating in June — creating a concrete near-term deadline for the problem

**Contradictions to Initial Hypothesis:**
- We assumed participants were the primary users; interviews revealed organizers are the more critical design target
- We assumed the problem was mainly about finding information; it's equally about the organizer's distribution burden

**New Questions Raised:**
- How do we design onboarding that gets organizers to switch from their existing multi-platform workflow?
- What is the minimum viable feature set that delivers value to both organizer and participant simultaneously?

### Design Phase Preparation

**Solution Design Constraints (from discovery):**
1. Must push notifications — pull-based solutions will fail (participants won't reliably check in)
2. Must require near-zero extra effort from the organizer — if it adds steps, it won't be adopted
3. Must provide a single source of truth — no multi-platform broadcasting

**Key User Needs to Address:**
1. Organizer needs one-action broadcast that reaches all participants automatically
2. Participants need direct push notifications for schedule changes, not buried chat messages
3. Both need a way to confirm game status (confirmed / TBC / cancelled) without ambiguity

**Initial Solution Direction:**
A lightweight, push-based sports coordination tool where the organizer posts once and all registered participants receive a direct notification. When anything changes, the update goes out automatically — no re-announcement cycle required.

## Risks & Mitigation

**Risk 1: Organizer adoption**
- Likelihood: ☑ High
- Impact: ☑ High
- Mitigation: Design organizer workflow first; solution must be simpler than current multi-platform posting cycle, not more complex

**Risk 2: Sandro graduating in June — group loses its coordinator**
- Likelihood: ☑ High
- Impact: ☑ High
- Mitigation: Build solution that is coordinator-agnostic — any member should be able to take over the organizer role without friction

**Risk 3: Low participant adoption if organizer doesn't switch**
- Likelihood: ☑ Medium
- Impact: ☑ High
- Mitigation: Cotne's bot proved this failure mode — solution must be designed so the organizer is the first and primary user, not participants
